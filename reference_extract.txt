Summary
This work describes a brain-computer interface that maps and reads neural activity. This is achieved
by using electrodes and electronics for signal acquisition and conditioning. The collected information
is then transmitted to any mobile device or computer. Transferred data is processed, enabling
advanced analysis and interpretation of neural signals. In this way, various devices and appliances
can be controlled using brain signals, opening completely new possibilities in scientific research and
for people with special needs in their daily lives.
During this work, a PCB, including ADC and MCU with a corresponding protection circuit,
adjustable amplification, was designed, manufactured, and assembled. A learning model was also
developed in Python using Random Forest that can identify the signals and translate them into mental
states such as eyes closed, eyes open, right-hand motor imagery and left-hand motor imagery and
these mental states are then used to control a mechatronics device.
There is also the exploration of life-changing possibilities for people with mobility disabilities and
cognitive problems. Among other contributions of this work are BCI technologies that will be more
adaptable for research, students, and developers. This work contributes to the accessibility of
neurotechnologies; this will enable users from diverse backgrounds to explore new affordable
technologies.

Introduction
For a long time, controlling objects with our mind was a popular idea limited to fantasy in science
fiction and cartoons; today, thanks to the advancement of technology, that idea may become a reality.
Brain-computer interfaces (BCIs) are systems that provide a direct connection between the brain and
an external device. BCI technologies have emerged as a transformative area of research linking
machine control with human cognition. BCI technology has immense applications in different fields.
For example, in medicine, BCIs are used for amputees to facilitate their usage of prosthetics. They
are also used to prevent muscle atrophy in rehabilitation of fully or partially paralytic patients. In the
entertainment industry, BCIs are popular for video games, enhancing user experience. BCIs also
facilitate the usage and development of robotics. This topic is relevant and necessary to be addressed
due to the growing demand for solutions that require advanced human-machine interaction, with the
main goals of improving the quality of life for individuals with motor disabilities or innovative
technological advancement. BCI technology has undergone several stages of development on both
hardware and software components from its initial stages of electroencephalography (EEG), a noninvasive neuroimaging technique that measures electrical activity in the brain using electrodes placed
on the scalp, to the usage of high-precision analog-to-digital converters (ADCs), efficient
communication protocols such as Serial Peripheral Interface (SPI) and WIFI, and the advancement
of more intricate algorithms. The advancement of Artificial Intelligence (AI) and machine learning
(ML) has accelerated software evolution. ML algorithms such as linear discriminant analysis (LDA),
random forests and decision trees enable data processing from neural signals to recognize motor
activity. Reinforcement learning improves the use of these algorithms, polishing signal processing
and creating sharper interfaces. Current BCI systems are taking big steps in controlling devices.
Nevertheless, there are challenges that need to be faced. Among them is the need for the development
of better systems and low-cost solutions. Current BCIs are complicated, closed-sourced, and
exclusive; this limits who can work on them and slows down the overall progress in the field.
Additionally, testing such systems is a delicate topic because of the biological nature of the device.
One encounters issues in signal processing, noise or interference. The solution would be to use highquality sensors; however, this leads to another challenge. Obtaining such high sensitivity sensors can
be costly, and it can be hard to find such sensors available to regular consumers. Despite these
challenges, research on BCIs must prevail as their benefits outweigh disadvantages.
The main aim of this project is to develop a brain-computer interface that acquires, processes, and
utilizes neuroelectric signals to control a mechatronic device.

1. Situation Analysis
Human-computer interfaces (HCIs) are systems including hardware and software that receive inputs
from a human user and translate them into actions or applications via software. HCI refers to a broad
spectrum of technologies; however, this section will focus on current or proposed HCIs governed by
the user's cerebral activity [1].
Brain-computer interface (BCI) is a developing technique within the field of neuroscience. BCI
technology enables a direct interface between the brain and external equipment, bypassing the
neuromuscular pathway. BCI not only serves the medical area and healthcare but also plays a part in
several other domains of human existence, including entertainment, gaming, education, selfregulation, and marketing. Alongside its advantages, BCI also presents drawbacks that might be
categorized as technological, neurological, and ethical. This review study examines the fundamental
concepts of noninvasive brain-computer interfaces (BCI), brain signals, and their components [2].
1.1. BCI System Architecture
The BCI system consists of three essential components, each fulfilling distinct functions: signal
acquisition, signal processing, and application (Fig. 1). These components are interlinked and
function collaboratively to facilitate the transmission of neural impulses to the designated BCI
application (e.g., robotic arm). In specific circumstances, control signals from the BCI application
may be transmitted to the brain to elicit certain fundamental human functions, such as vision and
hearing [2, 3].

Fig. 1. Main elements of a brain-computer interface (BCI) system [3].

During the initial stage of signal acquisition, brain activity is recorded with the help of sensorselectrodes that map the brain in different areas such as the scalp or implanted directly in the brain.
The signal processing stage combines filtering, feature extraction, feature classification that are done
specifically for the user final purpose, lastly there is the BCI application stage, this part inputs the
user intentions, movements or thoughts and outputs commands for devices such as computer cursors,
wheelchairs and robotic arms [4].
1.2. Signal Acquisition
This is the first element of the BCI system, which recognizes and quantifies brain impulses. This
component is primarily responsible for receiving and registering signals produced by neural activity
and transmitting these signals to the subsequent component of the BCI (preprocessing phase) for
signal enhancement and electrical noise reduction [5]. Quantifying brain-generated oscillations is a
fundamental aspect of any BCI-based technology. It denotes the voluntary brain responses elicited
13

by the user's current actions. Diverse techniques for signal acquisition have been examined. The BCI
application and its primary user category determine the appropriate signal acquisition method and the
phenomena to be measured. (Fig. 2) illustrates categories of brain acquisition techniques: invasive
and non-invasive methods. Invasive technology involves the neurosurgical implantation of electrodes
into the user's brain or on its surface, whereas non-invasive technologies utilize external sensors to
assess brain activity.

Fig. 2. BCI sensor mounting types: invasive (IM), semi-invasive (ECoG), and noninvasive (MEG, EEG,

1.2.1. Non-Invasive BCIs
Non-invasive methods have effectively facilitated severely and partially paralyzed individuals by
regaining fundamental communication abilities and operating neuroprocessing and wheelchairs.
Despite the remarkable efficacy of non-invasive techniques in BCI applications, motor recovery has
been constrained by the necessity for higher-resolution brain signals [2]. Invasive recording methods,
such as electrocorticography and intracortical neuron recording, are employed to improve the quality
of brain signals monitored via brain-computer interfaces (BCIs) [2]. Most researchers concur that the
restoration of movement by prosthesis with numerous degrees of freedom can alone be accomplished
through invasive methods. The enhancement of efficacy of non-invasive techniques is improbable in
the near future. Thus, invasive techniques seem essential for precise control of neuroprocessing.
Nonetheless, this matter remains ambiguous, and several perspectives contradict this hypothesis,
including this study [2, 3, 7]. These recording technologies adhere to an approach that avoids the
implantation of external elements into the subject's brain. Consequently, it circumvents the surgical
interventions or permanent device implantation required by intrusive acquisition [8]. This recording
method is the most used because of its portability and safety regards [9]. There are three main
electrode-sensor types as shown in Fig. 3:
1. Wet electrodes work by applying conductive gel to reduce skin-electrode impedance. This
requires preparation to be used but results in high signal fidelity.
2. Dry electrodes work without conductive gel; this is favorable for a quick setup, but on the other
hand, signal quality is less than that of wet electrodes.
3. Gold or Ag/AgCl-coated electrodes are the most widely used due to their biocompatibility; if used
with gel, they behave as wet electrodes but with the advantage of reusability and durability [10].

Other non-invasive recording modalities are functional near-infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS),
functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI), and magnetoencephalography (MEG), which
measures hemodynamic or magnetic correlates of neural activity. These complement EEG but at a
high cost or are stationary, which limits their use in BCI everyday applications [9, 10].
1.2.2. Invasive BCIs
Invasive brain-computer interfaces are more sensitive due to the involved surgical intervention by
medical professionals to implant microelectrodes directly on or inside the cortex.
Electrocorticography (ECoG) involves placing flexible grids on the pial surface; this method achieves
high spatial resolution (millimeters) and higher signal-noise ratios than regular EEG. This method
avoids the penetration of cortical tissue. Intracortical microelectrodes penetrate the cortex and record
single units, which reduces noise at the cost of surgical risk, system longevity, and potential tissue
response to external material.
Extensive clinical iterations have shown that intracortical arrays are able to support multi-degree-offreedom robotic arm control and handwriting-to-text communication at high speeds [6]. But on the
other hand, it requires neurosurgery, clinical oversights, and lifelong maintenance.
Fig. 4 demonstrates an application of an invasive BCI where users performed several iterations

4b and 4c show the raw signals for specific letters that would later be translated into the drawing
board; Fig. 4d and Fig. 4e describes the output from the board after several iterations and describes

Upon the acquisition of neural data as raw signals, these are transformed into control commands. As
presented in Fig. 5. There is a process pipeline which consists of three stages: feature extraction,
feature classification, and feature translation [12].

1.3.1. Feature Extraction
The initial stage of signal processing is referred to as “feature extraction”, which seeks to characterize
EEG signals using a limited number of essential values known as features. Such features should
encapsulate the information inherent in EEG signals pertinent to characterizing mental states while
excluding noise and extraneous data. All extracted features are typically organized into a vector
known as a feature vector. For instance, consider a Motor Imagery (MI)-based Brain-Computer
Interface (BCI), which can identify imagined movements such as those of the left or right hand as
shown in Fig. 5 [1, 7, 12, 13].
Inside features for extraction include band power for specific ranges (alpha, beta and gamma bands);
others are also called temporal features due to their event-related potentials (P300, N200) and spatial
patterns for multiple electrodes [9]. The aim of these features is to reduce dimensions of the data
while keeping its relevant information. Technologies such as common spatial patterns (CSP) and time
analysis (short-time Fourier transform) are frequently used for the discriminative power of the
features.

recent models such as Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) showcase better performance due to
their learning complex patterns with data [14].

2.1.1. Analog-to-Digital Converter (ADC) Selection
Analog-to-digital converters (ADCs) are essential elements in biomedical, communications, and
signal processing systems that consume low power and have excellent conversion efficiency, serving
to transform biopotential signals into digital signals for processing purposes. ADCs are devices that
enable one-way conversion from analogue to digital signals domain. The implementation of ADCs
is executed as either serial or parallel converters. Prior to translating a signal to the digital domain, an
analog signal is initially transformed into discrete form via a sample and hold circuit. If the sample is
simultaneously compared to several quantization levels and converted to the digital domain in one
step, it is referred to as a parallel converter. If the system sequentially compares the sampled output
to various quantization levels, it is referred to as a Serial Converter. An example of a parallel converter
is the flash converter, while the serial converter includes single, dual slope, and successive
approximation register (SAR) types [17].
High Performance Converters are a specialized category of ADC distinguished by enhanced features,
including sampling rates in the megahertz range and resolutions exceeding 16 bits [17].
There are different types of ADC converters among which these were analyzed; Flash, Counter,
Single Slope, Dual Slope, SAR, Sigma Delta, Pipelined, Interleaving.
Flash analog-to-digital converters
The fastest way to convert an analogue signal into a digital signal is flash ADC, or parallel ADCs.
Though often limited to 8-bit resolution, flash ADCs are best for applications requiring great
bandwidth since they consume more power than other ADC designs. As shown in (Fig. 7). When the
analog input lies between VX4 and VX5, comparators X1 to X4 output 1s, while the other
comparators yield 0s [8, 18].

Fig. 7. The architecture of flash ADC [19].

Counter analog-to-digital converter
A DAC, a digital counter, an AND gate, and a comparator make up this kind of ADC. Starting at
zero, the digital counter every clock pulse increases the value of the digital counter by a step,
transforms it to analogue form, and then compares it to the analogue input. The AND gate acts as a
control signal. The digital counter's value rises to match the sampled input. The final value shows at
the output [17-19].

Fig. 8. The block diagram of Ramp converter [17, 19].

A SAR ADC takes an analogue input signal into a digital format using the ramp approach. What
differs is that instead of using a digital counter starting at zero, the ADC uses a synthetic
approximation register started counting from the most significant bit, as shown in (Fig. 9) [17-20].

2.1.5. PCB Design: Technical Justification
There are various ways to design a PCB depending on the application; to simplify the design
components, could be Through Holes Technology (THT) or Surface-Mount Technology (SMT).
Through-Hole Components. This technology works by placing components through holes in the
PCB and then solder is applied on the opposite side. This method has advantages such as rapid for
prototyping and good mechanical strength, also it has drawbacks when it comes to sensitive
biomedical devices.
Surface-Mount Technology (SMT). This technology works by mounting components directly on
the PCB’s surface layer, this removes the need for drilling of holes, there are other advantages for
which this BCI will be SMT. Other advantages such as shorter signal pathways since SMT
components are often shorter in their leads (1<mm) this minimizes parasitic inductance and
capacitance. Lower noise crosstalk because shorter traces help to reduce EMI pickup and crosstalk
between communication channels. This improves signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). Lightweight design –
SMT improves the design by decreasing the PCB size which is crucial for a wearable and comfortable
BCI. Better thermal performance. SMT components usually have a possibility of heat dissipation
trough PCB itself [32].

bands (delta, theta, alpha, beta, gamma) in the EEG spectrum. This main filtering method uses Fast
Fourier Transform (FFT). A fast Fourier transform (FFT) is an efficient approach for calculating the
discrete Fourier transform (DFT) of an input vector. The FFT efficiently computes the DFT of an nelement vector in O (n log n) operations, whereas the FFT computed requires O (n²) operations. Fast
Fourier Transforms (FFTs) are applicable to any vector length n and may accommodate real and
multidimensional data. Parallel FFTs have been created since the emergence of parallel computing
[36]. In this program (FFT) was implemented as shown in (Fig. 32).

3.7. Machine Learning Model Development
The purpose of developing a learning model was to differentiate with live data four distinguish mental
states which are: Eyes open, Eyes closed, Right hand up, Left hand up. The reason why these four
states were selected was because of the neurophysiological footprint and reliable use for controlling
devices.
for validating this learning model, the following data was generated using Python script and it consists
of:
− 1200 samples (300 samples per mental state);
− 8 channels (ADC – converted to FFT);
− 5 Frequency bands per channel.
Random Forest classification algorithm was selected due to its good performance for high
dimensional feature spaces, ability to handle nonlinear type of data, classification of data reaches high
accuracy levels, its resist overfitting through ensemble learning. The training procedure for this
Random Forest model occupied 80 percent of the data generated for training and 20 percent for
validating the trained model. There were 100 decision trees in the ensemble, also a maximum of 10
depth for each tree. With the Python generated data script, the model achieved 92 % accuracy which
implies it is excellent along all four mental states.

To prevent false triggers a mechanism called confidence threshold was implemented as follows
Classification probability only triggers if it is above 0.85 and hence register a state change, each state
should be maintained for at least 1 second to confirm the change in state, also a hysteresis mechanism
was implemented to avoid rapid state changing.
